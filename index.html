Pre-Adoption Birth

Every birth parent deserves compassionate support and clear information about their options.

What is Pre-Adoption Birth Support?

Pre-adoption birth services provide emotional, medical, and legal support for individuals considering adoption for their child. These services ensure that birth parents understand their rights, receive proper prenatal care, and have access to counseling throughout the process.

Your Rights as a Birth Parent

  • Right to receive unbiased information about adoption options.
  • Right to choose the adoptive family or agency.
  • Right to legal representation and counseling.
  • Right to withdraw consent within a specified timeframe (varies by state).
  • Right to receive post-placement support and medical care.

Resources in Rhode Island

RI Adoption Helpline: 1-800-555-1234
RI Legal Services: 401-274-2652
Adoption Counseling: 401-421-5790
Prenatal Care: 1-800-942-7434
If you are considering adoption, speak with a trusted counselor or social worker to understand all your options. You are not alone.

Addiction Is A Disease

Addiction is a chronic brain disease, not a moral failing. Recovery is possible with treatment and support.

What Is Addiction?

Addiction is a treatable, chronic medical disease involving complex interactions among brain circuits, genetics, the environment, and an individual's life experiences. People with addiction use substances or engage in behaviors that become compulsive and often continue despite harmful consequences.

Signs of Addiction

  • Loss of control over substance use
  • Cravings and preoccupation
  • Neglecting responsibilities
  • Withdrawal from family and friends
  • Tolerance (needing more to feel effects)
  • Continued use despite harm

Treatment & Recovery

  • Call SAMHSA National Helpline: 1-800-662-4357 (confidential, 24/7)
  • Rhode Island Buprenorphine & Naloxone Helpline: 401-606-5456
  • Local support: Alcoholics Anonymous, Narcotics Anonymous, SMART Recovery
  • Medications for opioid use disorder (methadone, buprenorphine, naltrexone) are effective.
samhsa.gov/find-help
RI Department of Behavioral Healthcare
401-462-4691
Recovery is possible. Compassion and support save lives. If you or someone you love is struggling, reach out today.

How to Respond to an Overdose

Every second counts. Know the signs and act quickly.

Recognize an Overdose

  • Person is unresponsive or won't wake up
  • Breathing is slow, shallow, or stopped
  • Blue or gray lips/fingertips
  • Choking or gurgling sounds
  • Limp body, pale skin

Steps to Respond

1. Call 911

Give clear location. Tell them the person is not breathing or unresponsive. Rhode Island's Good Samaritan Law protects you and the person from prosecution for drug possession.

2. Administer Naloxone (Narcan)

If available, use naloxone nasal spray or injectable. It reverses opioid overdoses with no harmful effects if given to someone not overdosing. Free naloxone is available at many pharmacies and community organizations.

3. Perform Rescue Breathing

Tilt head back, lift chin, pinch nose, give one breath every 5 seconds. Continue until person breathes on their own or EMS arrives.

4. Stay with the Person

Place them in the recovery position (on side) if they are breathing but unconscious. Stay until help arrives.

Where to Get Naloxone

Participating pharmacies (no prescription needed)
Community overdose prevention programs
RI Naloxone Hotline: 401-222-5960
You can save a life. Carry naloxone, learn the signs, and act immediately.

Perinatal Substance Use Trifold Brochure

Support for a healthy pregnancy and baby starts with compassion, not judgment.

Why It Matters

Substance use during pregnancy can affect both mother and baby. But with proper care, treatment, and support, families can achieve healthy outcomes. Stigma often prevents women from seeking help – we are here to support you.

Potential Risks

For the Baby

  • Preterm birth and low birth weight
  • Neonatal abstinence syndrome (NAS)
  • Developmental delays

For the Mother

  • High blood pressure and preeclampsia
  • Infections (HIV, hepatitis)
  • Mental health challenges

Get Support – No Judgment

  • Project RESPECT: Integrated prenatal care and substance use treatment at Women & Infants Hospital – 401-274-1122
  • RI Parent Support Network: 401-467-6855
  • Substance Use Helpline for Pregnant Women: 1-800-662-4357

Tips for a Healthy Pregnancy

Early prenatal care
Medication-assisted treatment (MAT) is safe
Attend all pediatric appointments
Breastfeeding support if appropriate
You are not alone. Asking for help is a sign of strength. A healthy future for you and your baby is possible.

3 Preparedness Steps

Being prepared can save lives. Start with these three steps.

1. Make a Plan

  • Discuss with family how you will contact each other.
  • Choose two meeting places: one near home, one outside the neighborhood.
  • Know evacuation routes and shelter locations.
  • Include pets in your plan.

2. Build a Kit

Basic Supplies

  • Water (1 gallon per person per day, 3 days)
  • Non-perishable food (3 days)
  • Flashlight, batteries, radio
  • First aid kit

Additional Items

  • Medications and medical supplies
  • Important documents (copies)
  • Cash, phone charger, blankets
  • Diapers, wipes, baby formula if needed

3. Stay Informed

  • Sign up for RI Emergency Alerts: riema.ri.gov
  • Know your local radio stations for emergency updates.
  • Monitor weather forecasts and community advisories.

What is a Community Health Worker

CHWs are trusted members of the community who connect people to health care and social services.

Role of a Community Health Worker

A Community Health Worker (CHW) is a frontline public health worker who is a trusted member of the community they serve. They bridge gaps between health services and individuals, providing culturally appropriate support, health education, and navigation assistance.

How CHWs Help

  • Schedule medical appointments
  • Arrange transportation to appointments
  • Provide nutrition and wellness coaching
  • Help with insurance applications
  • Offer language interpretation
  • Link to housing, food, and employment resources

Find a CHW in Rhode Island

  • Many community health centers and clinics employ CHWs.
  • Contact the RI Community Health Worker Association: richwa.org
  • Call 2-1-1 and ask for CHW services in your area.
Interested in becoming a CHW? Training available through local colleges and health departments.
CHWs are your neighbors, helping you navigate the health system. Reach out – they are here for you.

Carbon Monoxide Poisoning

EACH YEAR HUNDREDS OF PEOPLE IN THE UNITED STATES DIE FROM UNINTENTIONAL EXPOSURE TO CARBON MONOXIDE POISONING.

WHAT IS CARBON MONOXIDE POISONING?

Carbon Monoxide (CO) is a gas produced by burning any type of fuel – gas, oil, kerosene, wood, or charcoal. It's invisible and odorless. Breathing too much can be deadly.

Know how to prevent carbon monoxide poisoning!

Installation & Maintenance

  • Read and follow instructions when installing appliances.
  • Always maintain appliances according to manufacturer's recommendations.
  • Install CO detectors in several areas of your home.
  • Test CO alarms routinely and replace batteries.

CO Alarms Reminder

CO alarms provide peace of mind but are not a substitute for proper installation, use, and maintenance of appliances.

SOURCES OF CARBON MONOXIDE

Exhaust from auto or generator in garage

Room heater, malfunctioning range, gas oven

Generator exhaust in home, basement, or crawlspace

Rusting or water streaking on vent/chimney

Loose masonry on chimney, sooting, debris falling

Know the signs of CO poisoning!

Initial symptoms:

  • Headache
  • Dizziness
  • Nausea
  • Fatigue
  • Shortness of breath

Additional signs:

  • Headaches only while indoors
  • You feel better when outdoors
  • Others have same symptoms
What to do if you have symptoms:
  • Leave area immediately, get fresh air.
  • If severe, call 911 from another location.
  • Call fire department to check CO level.
  • Contact Poison Center 800-222-1222.
  • See doctor if symptoms persist.
UNVENTED HEATERS ARE UNSAFE!

Resources

Health Equity Zones – Rhode Island

80% of health is determined outside the doctor's office — in our homes, schools, jobs, and neighborhoods.

WHAT ARE HEALTH EQUITY ZONES?

Health Equity Zones (HEZs) are place-based initiatives that bring communities together to build healthy, resilient communities. Led by residents and community partners, each HEZ identifies unique social, economic, and environmental factors affecting health and implements data-driven action plans to address them.

DETERMINANTS OF HEALTH

What shapes our health?

  • Health Behaviors: 30%
  • Genes & Clinical Care: 20%
  • Social/Economic/Environmental Factors: 50%

Based on Tarlov & Kindig frameworks

Equity vs Equality

Equality gives everyone the same resources. Equity gives each community what they need to have a fair chance at good health. Place matters.

IMPACT ACROSS RHODE ISLAND

Mental Health First Aid: Washington County HEZ trained 1,000+ police, clergy, teachers.
Complete & Green Streets: Pawtucket/CF HEZ helped pass first statewide ordinance for safe streets.
Walking School Bus: Olneyville HEZ boosted school attendance.
Smoke-Free Parks: Bristol HEZ banned smoking/vaping in all town parks.
Diversion to Treatment: West Warwick HEZ embedded clinician with police for substance use.
Housing Advocacy: Southside HEZ advanced equitable housing policy.

Some Facts About Bed Bugs

Bed bugs are hitchhikers — they travel in luggage, clothing, and used furniture.

WHAT ARE BED BUGS?

Bed bugs are small, flat insects that feed on blood. Adults are about 1/4 inch (size of a pencil width), reddish-brown. They hide in cracks, mattresses, box springs, and headboards. Active at night, they live months without feeding.

WHY ARE THEY MAKING A COMEBACK?

Less use of harsh chemicals and increased travel have allowed bed bugs to spread. They are excellent hitchhikers and can infest any home regardless of cleanliness.

HEALTH EFFECTS

Bites & Reactions

  • Raised red welts, itching
  • Sleeplessness, anxiety
  • Feces/casings can trigger asthma

Disease Risk

No evidence bed bugs transmit bloodborne diseases. However, scratching can lead to skin infections.

HOW TO SPOT AN INFESTATION

  • Raised red bites on family members
  • Live bed bugs (small, flat, brown)
  • Rusty or reddish spots on sheets
  • Tiny eggs in cracks/crevices
  • Shed skins or musty odor

HOW TO CONTROL BED BUGS

Professional Help

Hire a licensed pest control professional. Call RI DEM at 401-222-2781 for a list of licensed professionals.

Landlord responsibility: If infestation is in two or more units or common areas, owner must treat. Contact local Minimum Housing if unresolved.

Do-It-Yourself Tips

  • Wash clothes/sheets in hot water & dry on high heat
  • Encase mattresses & pillows in plastic covers
  • Discard heavily infested furniture
  • Vacuum thoroughly and seal cracks

Some Facts About Mold

Mold grows where moisture is present — leaky roofs, damp basements, bathrooms, and areas with poor ventilation.

WHAT IS MOLD?

Mold is a type of fungus that thrives in damp, humid conditions. It reproduces through tiny spores that travel through the air. Mold can grow on walls, ceilings, fabrics, carpets, and furniture. While most molds are harmless, some can cause health problems and damage your home.

HEALTH EFFECTS OF MOLD

Respiratory issues

  • Nasal stuffiness, sneezing
  • Cough, wheezing
  • Asthma triggers
  • Eye & skin irritation

Who is at higher risk?

  • Children & infants
  • People with asthma/allergies
  • Immunocompromised individuals
  • Elderly adults

HOW TO PREVENT MOLD GROWTH

Control Moisture

  • Use exhaust fans in bathrooms & kitchen
  • Fix leaks promptly
  • Keep humidity below 50%
  • Ensure proper drainage around house

Clean & Ventilate

  • Open windows when possible
  • Clean moldy surfaces with detergent & water
  • Remove wet materials (carpets, drywall) if not dried within 48h

MOLD CLEANUP GUIDELINES

  • Wear N-95 mask, gloves, and goggles
  • Open windows for ventilation
  • Scrub hard surfaces with detergent and water, then dry completely
  • Throw away porous materials (ceiling tiles, carpet) if moldy
  • For large infestations (>10 sq ft), consider professional help
